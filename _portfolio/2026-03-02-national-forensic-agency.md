## Executive Summary

Nigeria’s current forensic landscape is fragmented, underfunded, and largely dependent on ad-hoc arrangements. This fragmentation compromises the chain of custody, delays justice, and raises critical issues regarding **genomic sovereignty**. 

This 14-page policy brief argues for the urgent establishment of a **National Forensic Agency (NFA)**—an independent, statutory body responsible for standardizing forensic practices, managing national DNA databases, and coordinating forensic expertise across all tiers of government.

## Key Highlights from the Brief

*   **The Problem:** Current forensic services are scattered across police commands and university labs with no unified regulatory framework, leading to inconsistent evidence standards and vulnerable data handling.
*   **The Solution:** An NFA would serve as the central hub for forensic science, operating with administrative independence to ensure impartiality, technical excellence, and ethical governance.
*   **Genomic Sovereignty:** The brief details how a centralized agency can keep sensitive biological data within Nigeria, preventing external exploitation and aligning with the Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023.
*   **Strategic Financing:** Recommendations on leveraging international "Blend" country financing to build infrastructure without overburdening the national budget.
*   **Legislative Roadmap:** Specific amendments required for the Evidence Act 2011 and the proposed NFA Bill to ensure legal robustness.

## Why This Matters Now

As Nigeria advances its digital and scientific infrastructure, the lack of a centralized forensic authority remains a bottleneck for both national security and human rights protection. This brief provides a roadmap for policymakers, legislators, and international partners to support this critical institutional reform.
